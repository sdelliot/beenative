Welcome to Bee Native! Our goal is to provide you with accurate, useful, and quick-glance information about plants native to North Carolina to enable with education of native plants for gardners and native plant nuseries. This application is desiged for NC residents whom want a quick guide to understanding key gardening information about a plant. We make no such attempt to replace the high-quality field-botany plant identification guides provided by [UNC Chapel Hill Herbarium's FloraQuest](https://ncbg.unc.edu/research/unc-herbarium/flora-apps/). Rather we carefully gather and combine details from trusted botanical sources and provide a simplified lookup for garnders and NC plant nuseries. We are deeply grateful to the incredible resources of our data sources and recommend users interested in learning more about a particular plant to visit the cited sources.

This guide explains exactly where our data comes from, how we decide which plants are included, and the exact rules we use to organize everything so it's easy for you to understand.

## 1. Where Does the Data Come From?

To build a truly comprehensive guide, we "listen" to four of the most respected voices in North Carolina botany and horticulture:

* [**Vascular Plants of North Carolina**](https://auth1.dpr.ncparks.gov/flora/index.php): This is our "source of truth" for confirming which plants are truly native to our state. Maintained by the [NC Biodiversity Project](https://nc-biodiversity.com/), this essential resource is a complete compendium of all vascular plant species, subspecies, and varieties known to exist in North Carolina. Beyond simple identification, it offers insights into how common or rare a plant is—both locally and globally—alongside its legal state and federal conservation status. Lastly, it provides detailed information on its geographic range, preferred habitats, and bloom periods, as well as a county-level map showing exactly where in North Carolina the plant has been documented.
* [**NC State Extension Gardener Plant Toolbox**](https://plants.ces.ncsu.edu/): Guided by the principle of "right plant, right place," this innovative toolbox is designed to help you select plants that will thrive in your specific landscape while bringing you year-round joy. The database is a collaborative achievement built on global research and surveys from Extension agents, Master Gardener volunteers, and landscape professionals. It offers a wealth of details, including research-backed **pollinator advantages**, **toxicity alerts** for pet and child safety and **detailed attributes** ranging from soil pH preferences to salt tolerance. This data source was a driving inspiration for Bee Native, where we leverage a subset of their carefully curated information.

* [**Prairie Moon Nursery**](https://www.prairiemoon.com/): Rooted in the early American prairie restoration movement, Prairie Moon Nursery carries forward the "land ethic" championed by legendary conservationist Aldo Leopold. They offer a large collection of authentic, wild-type native plants, prioritizing original species over mass-market cultivars to preserve local genetics. For Bee Native users, Prairie Moon is our premier specialist for **germination codes**—essential for those starting their gardens from seed. Their deep expertise also enriches our database with verified **life-cycle data**, precise **bloom windows**, and reliable **moisture and height ranges**, while their specialized knowledge of **pollinator advantages** helps you choose plants that offer the greatest benefit to our local ecosystem.

* [**Flora of the Southeastern United States**](https://fsus.ncbg.unc.edu/): Hosted by the North Carolina Botanical Garden, this world-class botanical resource allows us to provide high-quality, scientifically accurate imagery for our guide. By integrating this database, we ensure that the photos you see are faithful representations of each plant, complete with professional photographer credits and ethical licensing information. For users seeking a deep-dive botanical experience, we highly recommend their [FloraQuest](https://ncbg.unc.edu/research/unc-herbarium/flora-apps/) app suite, which offers comprehensive field-guide tools for the entire southeastern region.

## 2. How Do We Select Native Plants?

It is incredibly important to us that Bee Native only shows plants that naturally belong in North Carolina's ecosystem. To achieve this, we follow a strict selection process based on the official color-coding system from the [**Vascular Plants of North Carolina**](https://auth1.dpr.ncparks.gov/flora/index.php).

When we analyze the state's plant list, we look at the background color of each entry. These colors tell us the "State Rank" and conservation status of a plant:

* **The Green Standard (Included):** We only include plants with a **light green** background. This color indicates "Native Taxa"—plants that are officially known to be original to North Carolina.
* **The "Non-Native" Filter (Excluded):** Our system automatically filters out and excludes any plants marked with the following colors, as they are not considered conservation targets in our state:
    * **Salmon/Orange (Exotic):** Plants that are not native to NC, including those from other continents or those that have "escaped" into the state from elsewhere.
    * **Yellow (Uncertain):** Species reported to be in the state, but whose presence has not been officially confirmed.
    * **Light Brown (Not Valid):** Taxa that are no longer considered valid names or have been merged into other scientific names.
    * **Lilac (Not in NC):** Plants that were once reported in North Carolina but have since been proven not to exist within our borders.
    * **Light Blue (Hybrids):** Hybrid plants without specific scientific status, which are generally not prioritized for native conservation.

By strictly adhering to these categories, Bee Native ensures that every plant you discover is a verified member of North Carolina's natural heritage.

## 3. How We Combine Information (Smart Mapping)

Because our various sources often use different terminology to describe the same traits, Bee Native uses "Smart Mapping" to simplify and standardize the information. This ensures that when you filter for a specific need, the results are consistent and easy to read.

### Sunlight Requirements
We translate various light descriptions from the **NC Plant Toolbox** and **Prairie Moon Nursery** into four standardized categories:
* **Full Sun:** Mapped from "Full sun" or "6 or more hours of direct sunlight."
* **Partial Shade:** Mapped from "Part sun," "Semi-shade," or "2–6 hours of sunlight."
* **Dappled Sunlight:** Mapped from "Filtered" light through tree leaves (from the **NC Plant Toolbox**).
* **Deep Shade:** Mapped from "Full shade," "Shade," or "Deep shade."

### Moisture & Soil
We analyze soil drainage descriptions from the **NC Plant Toolbox** and combine them with moisture details from **Prairie Moon Nursery**:
* **Wet:** Plants that handle "Frequent Standing Water" or "Occasional Flooding."
* **Medium-Wet:** Plants for "Moist" or "Occasionally Wet" soil.
* **Medium:** Standard garden soil described as having "Good Drainage."
* **Medium-Dry:** Plants for soil that is "Occasionally Dry."
* **Dry:** Plants that thrive in "Very Dry" or sandy conditions.

### Plant Categories
To prevent confusion, if a plant is identified as a **Tree, Shrub, or Vine**, it is barred from being labeled as a "Forb" (Wildflower). This ensures a large Oak tree is never accidentally categorized as a small wildflower just because a source mentions it is a perennial.
* **Trees:** Identified by keywords like *canopy, conifer, pine, fir.*
* **Shrubs:** Identified by keywords like *bush, multi-stemmed.*
* **Vines:** Identified by keywords like *climbing, liana.*
* **Grasses & Sedges:** Identified by keywords like *sedge, rush, graminoid.*
* **Ferns:** Identified by keywords like *frond, spore.*
* **Forbs (Wildflowers):** Identified by keywords like *wildflower, herb, perennial.*

### Bloom Colors
Botanical sources often use specific descriptive shades. We scan these descriptions and group them into 10 primary colors for easy filtering:
* **Red:** Includes crimson, scarlet, maroon, burgundy.
* **Yellow:** Includes gold, lemon, chartreuse, goldenrod.
* **Purple:** Includes lavender, mauve, lilac, violet, magenta.
* **Other standard colors:** White, Blue, Orange, Pink, Green, Brown, and Grey.

### Heights and Widths
Plant sizes can be written in many formats, such as "ft.", "inches", or symbols (e.g `"` ). Our system extracts these measurements and converts them all to decimal feet (e.g., "2.0–4.5 ft"). If sources disagree on a plant's size, we prioritize the data from **Prairie Moon Nursery**, as their records are specifically tuned to the natural growth patterns of wild-type native species.

### Bloom Times
Some sources list specific months like "May to Jul," while others use general seasons like "Spring." Our system translates seasons into specific month ranges (e.g., Spring becomes March, April, and May) and tallies the data from all sources to provide you with the most accurate bloom window possible.

### Common Names
Plants often have dozens of regional common names. We gather these from all our sources and remove "filler" words like "Most," "Some," or "Reference." The result is a clean, easy-to-read list of the names that North Carolinians actually use.

### Attractions and Resistances
We combine the simple "Yes/No" indicators from **Prairie Moon Nursery** (for bees, birds, or deer) with the rich descriptions from the **NC Plant Toolbox**. This creates two comprehensive lists for every plant:
* **Wildlife Value:** A list of what the plant attracts (e.g., butterflies, songbirds, specialized bees).
* **Resistances:** A list of what the plant tolerates (e.g., deer, salt, wind, or storm damage).

To better understand the "Attracts" information you see in the app, it is helpful to understand how this data is curated by the Plant Toolbox experts:

> The 'Attracts' part of the Plant Toolbox is an important feature... For some plants, we do not have good data about what the flowers attract, so we just say 'pollinators'. This might apply to a cultivar, for which we have data about the species but not about the specific cultivar of that species. 
>
> If we have data about a specific pollinator group (bees, butterflies, etc.) we'll use those specific terms. We also use 'pollinators' when the specific pollinator group is not one we can select to fill that field in the database (e.g., we don't yet have "flies" or "beetles" as options in the database...). Also, for flowers that attract all kinds of insects, we specify those we can and add 'pollinators' to signify an 'etcetera'.
>
> The 'Attracts' feature also applies to insects that use the plant as a larval food source, so *Cercis Canadensis* attracts butterflies, not as pollinators, but because it is a favorite food of certain caterpillars. 'Songbirds' is an option for the 'Attracts' field and refers to birds that are attracted to fruits **OR** to the caterpillars that feed on the plant...
>
> So the 'Attracts' field refers to many aspects of the plant's biology, not just pollination. We...also try to explain what the plant attracts in the 'Wildlife Value' field. There, we'll name specific butterflies, moths or native bees that use the plants.
>
> — **Scott Zona, Ph.D., NC State Extension Gardener Plant Toolbox**
